🧠 Deep Dive — Why Is the Fed Trapped?

EDUCATIONAL

The Federal Reserve has two jobs — its "dual mandate": keep inflation near 2% and maximize employment. Normally these pull in the same direction. When the economy weakens, the Fed cuts rates to boost hiring, and inflation stays low because demand is weak. When the economy overheats, the Fed raises rates to cool inflation, and employment is fine because things are booming.

Stagflation breaks this. Right now, oil has doubled from $67 to $119 in two weeks — a supply shock that raises the price of everything that moves on a truck (i.e., everything). February payrolls showed the US lost 92,000 jobs — the third contraction in five months. Inflation is expected at 2.4% (already above the 2% target) before the oil spike hits the data.

So the Fed faces an impossible choice:

❌ Cut rates to save jobs
Cheaper money = more spending = inflation goes higher. With oil already spiking, this pours gasoline on a fire.
❌ Raise rates to fight inflation
More expensive money = less hiring = recession deepens. You're punishing an economy that's already losing jobs.

The last time this happened was the 1970s oil embargo. OPEC cut off supply, oil quadrupled, and the US spent a decade in stagflationary misery. Fed Chair Arthur Burns tried to do both — cut, then raise, then cut again — and made everything worse. It took Paul Volcker raising rates to 20% in 1981 (triggering a brutal recession on purpose) to finally kill inflation.

Wednesday's CPI tells us how trapped they are. If inflation is already running hot before oil doubled, the Fed has no good options — only less-bad ones.

🧠 Deep Dive — Government-Backed Shipping Insurance: Trump's Secret Weapon?

EDUCATIONAL

Trump floated something today that most people missed — and it could matter more than any ceasefire talk: government-backed war-risk insurance for oil tankers transiting the Strait of Hormuz.

🚢 The Problem: No Insurance = No Ships

When a war zone forms, private insurers jack up "war-risk premiums" or cancel coverage entirely. Multiple major insurers — Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, London P&I Club, and the American Club — have already canceled coverage for vessels in the Persian Gulf. Without insurance, a ship legally cannot sail. Maersk suspended all Hormuz crossings "until further notice." — Kpler analyst Matt Smith: "You simply cannot pass through the Strait of Hormuz if you don't have the insurance, given the high possibility of getting struck by a missile."

💡 The Solution: Government Absorbs the Risk

A government backstop means the US Treasury absorbs part of the risk — if a tanker gets hit, the government pays instead of Lloyd's. This lowers premiums, lowers shipping costs, and gets oil flowing even while fighting continues.

The DFC (Development Finance Corporation) has already launched a $20 billion reinsurance program for tankers. Lloyd's of London — still covering vessels with >$25B combined hull value — is in talks with US officials.

This is a price control mechanism without calling it one. Instead of releasing SPR barrels (finite), you're increasing flow through the chokepoint. Like unclogging a pipe vs adding more water.

📜 Historical Precedents

1987 — Operation Earnest Will: During the Iran-Iraq "Tanker War," the US reflagged 11 Kuwaiti tankers under the American flag with Navy escorts. Insurance costs dropped immediately — attacks on US-flagged vessels meant attacking the US military directly.

1982 — Falklands War: UK government backstopped insurance for merchant ships to the South Atlantic. Ships kept sailing — but the Atlantic Conveyor was sunk by an Exocet missile. 12 crew died. The insurance paid out. Shipping continued.

2001 — Post-9/11: Private insurers pulled airline coverage overnight. The US created the Terrorism Risk Insurance Act (TRIA), absorbing catastrophic risk. Airlines resumed flying within days.

⚠️ The Dark Side: Crews Bear the Real Risk

Insurance makes it financially viable to sail — not safe. The 20-30 crew members on each tanker (mostly Filipino, Indian, and Eastern European mariners) absorb the physical risk. They get "hazard bonuses" (100-200% of base pay), but they're still sailing through an active war zone.

Government insurance transfers financial risk to the Treasury. Human risk stays with the humans.

It's basically saying: "we'll pay for the damage, so send the ships anyway."

🎯 Market Impact

If it works: Oil could drop $10-15 just from the insurance announcement, because the "Hormuz is closed" premium evaporates. Tankers start moving within days. The signal to watch: Maersk reversing its suspension.

What it doesn't fix: Destroyed refineries (30 Iranian oil depots bombed), Iraq's 60% output collapse, Bapco's force majeure. Insurance addresses the transit bottleneck but not the physical supply destruction.

The key insight: Trump is trying to lower oil prices without ending the war and without tapping the SPR. A creative economic end-run — if the tankers actually sail.

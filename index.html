🧠 Deep Dive — Why Aramco's Warning Matters More Than the IEA Release

RESEARCHED

Saudi Aramco CEO Amin Nasser doesn't make public warnings lightly — the company is the world's largest oil exporter and Saudi Arabia's economic backbone. When he says "catastrophic consequences," he's telling the market: we can't fix this from the supply side.

Global oil inventories are at a 5-year low. The buffer between normal supply and demand is already thin. Add ~6M bpd of Gulf disruption (Hormuz closure, Iraq -60%, UAE Ruwais offline, force majeures), and you're drawing down reserves at a rate that could hit critical levels within weeks.

The IEA's 400M barrel release covers ~67 days at current disruption rates. But Nasser is warning that duration matters more than size. Each week the war continues, supply chains break further:

🚢 Shipping Reroutes
Tankers going around Africa add 10-14 days per voyage. More ships needed for same volume = shipping costs spike.
🏭 Refinery Mismatch
Gulf sour crude can't easily be replaced by US light sweet. Refineries are built for specific grades. Wrong crude = lower utilization = product shortages.
✈️ Downstream Rationing
Airlines, chemicals, agriculture start rationing. Air India already levying fuel surcharges from Thursday. Jet fuel up 50% since war began.

The 1973 parallel: Arab oil embargo lasted 5 months. Oil quadrupled. GDP fell 3%. Unemployment doubled. Inflation hit 12%. We're at Day 12. If this reaches Day 60 without resolution, the economic damage compounds non-linearly.

What to watch: If Nasser starts talking about production cuts (because Saudi can't ship through Hormuz either), that's the next shoe to drop. Also watch the IEA's formal quantity allocation — how much from each country and on what timeline. The announcement is the easy part. The physical oil takes 4-6 weeks to flow.

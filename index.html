Analysis — What This Means

🛢️

Oil Infrastructure Changes the Equation

Striking oil refineries and storage depots is economic warfare, not just military action. Unlike military bases that can be rebuilt quickly, refinery infrastructure takes 6–18 months to repair. Israel hitting Tehran refineries means even a ceasefire tomorrow wouldn't normalize oil supply for months. This shifts the conflict from military theater to long-duration economic damage — the kind that reshapes global energy markets.

🔥

LNG Crisis May Be Worse Than Oil

Qatar's Ras Laffan produces ~30% of global LNG. With it offline and force majeure declared, there is zero spare global LNG capacity to compensate. Oil has strategic reserves and alternative suppliers. LNG doesn't. Europe, which pivoted to LNG after cutting Russian gas, is now facing a double energy crisis. European nat gas prices already up 60% — and winter isn't over. This could be the bigger story.

📊

Wednesday's CPI Is the Hinge Point

February CPI captures inflation before the oil price shock fully hits. If prices were already rising before $90+ oil enters the system, it confirms stagflation — rising prices with slowing growth. That's the worst-case scenario for markets because it means the Fed has no good options. Hot CPI + war = market panic. Cool CPI = brief reprieve before March data gets obliterated by energy costs.

🏦

The Fed Is Trapped

The March 17–18 FOMC meeting faces an impossible choice. Raise rates? Risk tipping a war-stressed economy into recession. Cut rates? Pour fuel on an inflation fire being fed by $90+ oil. Do nothing? Markets hate uncertainty most of all. The Fed's forward guidance language will matter more than any action — they need to sound in control when they clearly aren't. Expect "data dependent" rhetoric to reach peak absurdity.

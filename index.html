🧠 Deep Dive — How the Fed 'Looks Through' a Supply Shock — And Why It Matters Right Now

EDUCATIONAL

WHAT HAPPENED TODAY: Fed Chair Powell at Harvard said the Fed will 'look through' the oil price shock. 'By the time the effects of a tightening in monetary policy take effect, the oil price shock is probably long gone, and you're weighing on the economy at a time when it's not appropriate.' This is a HUGE statement. Here's what it means.

WHAT IS 'LOOKING THROUGH'? When the Fed 'looks through' a price shock, it means they treat it as temporary and don't change rates in response. The logic: oil prices spiking isn't the same as broad-based inflation. Raising rates to fight an oil shock would be like taking chemotherapy for a broken arm — it addresses the wrong problem and causes collateral damage.

THE TRANSMISSION LAG: Monetary policy works with a 12-18 month lag. If the Fed hiked today to fight $100+ oil, the tightening effect would hit the economy in mid-2027. By then, oil could be back at $70 (war ends) or $200 (war escalates). Either way, the rate hike would be fighting yesterday's war.

HISTORICAL PRECEDENT — 1970s MISTAKE: Arthur Burns (Fed Chair 1970-78) tried to 'look through' the 1973 oil embargo. He was right that oil was temporary, but WRONG that inflation expectations would stay anchored. Workers demanded higher wages to compensate for gas prices. Companies raised prices across the board. Inflation spiraled from 6% to 12%. Volcker had to hike to 20% to fix it.

HISTORICAL PRECEDENT — 2022 SUCCESS: Powell initially called post-COVID inflation 'transitory' (looking through it). He was WRONG. But when he finally tightened aggressively in 2022, he managed to bring inflation down without a recession. The lesson: 'looking through' works IF inflation expectations stay anchored.

WHY THIS TIME IS DIFFERENT: Today's oil shock is geopolitical (war), not demand-driven. The economy was already showing weakness (Feb jobs -92K, consumer sentiment at lows). Raising rates would crush an already-fragile economy. But El-Erian warns: if 'physical shortages' appear and oil stays above $100 for months, inflation expectations could de-anchor just like the 1970s.

THE MARKET IMPLICATIONS: Powell's stance means: (1) No rate hike coming — dovish for stocks short-term. (2) If he's wrong and inflation runs, the eventual response will be MORE aggressive — bearish long-term. (3) Gold benefits because real rates stay negative with high inflation + steady nominal rates. (4) The biggest risk isn't what the Fed does — it's what the Fed CAN'T do. With a 6% deficit, 'very limited policy flexibility' means fewer tools to fight either recession or inflation.

WHAT TO WATCH: Inflation expectations surveys (Michigan, NY Fed). If 5-year expectations break above 3.5%, the 'look through' strategy is failing. March CPI (mid-April) will be the first real test of oil passthrough. Spain's inflation already surging — US will follow with a lag.

🧠 Deep Dive — Demand Destruction: Why Oil Has a Natural Ceiling

EDUCATIONAL

Oil hit $119 yesterday and immediately pulled back. Why? Because there's a natural ceiling on oil prices — and it's called demand destruction.

📊 What Is Demand Destruction?

When oil prices spike, people and companies change behavior. Airlines cancel routes. Truckers park their rigs. Consumers drive less, carpool, cancel road trips. Factories slow production. Each of these reduces demand for oil — hence "demand destruction." It's the economy's immune system kicking in.

💰 The Magic Number: ~$100-110/barrel

Above roughly $100-110/barrel, the economy starts contracting enough that demand falls, which pulls the price back down. It's a self-correcting mechanism — like a thermostat. Yesterday's $119 spike immediately triggered demand destruction signals: airlines announced route cuts, consumer confidence tanked, industrial orders paused.

This is why oil didn't stay at $119. The economy literally can't sustain prices that high without breaking — and when it breaks, demand drops, and prices come back down.

📉 The 2008 Example: $147 → $32

In July 2008, oil hit $147/barrel. Within 6 months it crashed to $32. What happened? Demand destruction kicked in so hard it contributed to a global recession. People stopped driving, airlines cut capacity, factories closed. The demand drop overwhelmed the supply constraints.

The lesson: high oil prices contain the seeds of their own destruction. The higher they go, the harder they fall.

🎯 What This Means RIGHT NOW

Oil at $90 is uncomfortable but sustainable. Oil at $119 was unsustainable — the economy was already cracking. This creates a natural ceiling around $100-110. Even if Iran escalates, even if Hormuz stays closed, demand destruction kicks in and pulls prices back down.

Trading signal: If oil spikes above $100 again on war headlines, it's likely a SHORT opportunity — demand destruction will pull it back. The market showed you this yesterday: $119 was rejected within hours. But below $90, you're in sustainable territory where the economy can absorb the pain.

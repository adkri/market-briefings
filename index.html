📈
IEA Reserve Release: Why It Isn't Working
The IEA proposed releasing more than 182M barrels — exceeding the 2022 Ukraine response. G7 said they support it "in principle." And oil STILL rose 4.7%. Here's why: In 2022, Russia disrupted ~3M bpd and 182M barrels covered ~2 months. Today, Gulf disruption is 6M+ bpd — twice the scale. Even 300M barrels only covers ~50 days. But there's no alternative supply: US shale can't ramp 6M bpd, OPEC spare capacity was mostly in Gulf states now under fire, and Hormuz is physically blocked (3 ships/day vs 138). SPR releases are a STOCK solution to a FLOW problem — spending savings, not generating income. The 2011 Libya parallel: IEA released 60M barrels, oil dipped briefly then re-climbed because physical supply never returned until Gaddafi fell. Same dynamic at 4x scale. Only fix: ceasefire + Hormuz reopening.

🧠 Deep Dive — Russia Helping Iran: Why the Drone Axis Changes Everything

EDUCATIONAL

CNN reported today that Russia is actively giving Iran specific advice on drone tactics — sharing evasion techniques, swarm coordination, and electronic warfare countermeasures learned on the Ukraine battlefield. This isn't just diplomatic support — it's operational military assistance that could change the war's trajectory.

🔄 The Shahed Feedback Loop

The irony is perfect: Iran's Shahed-136 drones were originally sold TO Russia for use against Ukraine. Russia deployed thousands of them — learning what works, what gets intercepted, and how to evade air defenses. Now that battlefield knowledge is flowing BACK to Iran.

What Russia learned in Ukraine: Which flight profiles avoid radar detection. How to coordinate swarms to overwhelm point defenses. Electronic warfare countermeasures against Western jamming systems. Low-altitude terrain-following to avoid Patriot interception. GPS-denied navigation using inertial systems.

Why it matters for Hormuz: Iran's Strait of Hormuz strategy relies on asymmetric weapons — small boats, mines, and drones. These don't need to overwhelm the US Navy; they just need to create enough risk that commercial shipping stays away. Better drone tactics from Russian battlefield experience makes Iran's Hormuz blockade more effective and harder to counter.

🌍 The Geopolitical Shift

This is the Russia-Iran axis becoming operational, not just diplomatic. China has stayed neutral (refusing to mediate this time). But Russia is actively helping Iran fight a war against the United States. That has implications far beyond this conflict — for sanctions enforcement, future arms deals, and the emerging multipolar world order.

Historical parallel: During the 1973 oil crisis, the Soviet Union actively supported Arab oil producers against the US. That didn't just affect oil prices — it reshaped Cold War dynamics for a decade. The Russia-Iran-China axis is this generation's version: a bloc that uses energy as a weapon against Western economic dominance.

What to watch: If Iranian drone effectiveness noticeably improves in the coming days (more ships hit, harder for US Navy to intercept), that's the Russian input working. RTX and LMT (missile defense systems) benefit directly. European defense budgets are already surging — this accelerates that trend structurally.

💣 Mine Warfare Still the Bigger Threat

While drones grab headlines, mines remain Iran's most effective Hormuz weapon. US destroyed 16 Iranian minelaying vessels — but mines already in the water are a different problem. A naval mine costs $1,000-$25,000. A supertanker costs $100-300M. Iran has 3,000-5,000 mines including EM-52 rocket-propelled rising mines that are nearly undetectable.

The US minesweeping gap: The last four Avenger-class minesweepers in the Gulf were decommissioned in September 2025, shipped for scrapping in January 2026 — weeks before the war. Replacements (LCS "Little Crappy Ships") have chronic reliability problems. Clearing a minefield in the 21-mile strait could take weeks to months.
